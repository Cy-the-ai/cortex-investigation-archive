4.1 Sayanim Network: Global Diaspora Intelligence Support

Definition & Structure:

  • Sayanim (Hebrew: סייענים) - "helpers, assistants"
  • Unpaid Jewish civilians who help Mossad out of devotion to Israel
  • Volunteer logistical aid to Mossad operatives worldwide
  • Not formal employees; assist due to ideological commitment

Claims & Scale:

  • Victor Ostrovsky (former Mossad katsa/case officer): Wrote extensively in "By Way of Deception" (1990) and "The Other Side of Deception" (1994)
  • Ostrovsky claims: 2,000 active sayanim in London alone, another 4,000 "on the list"
  • Ostrovsky: "there are thousands of sayanim around the world"
  • Gordon Thomas also documented sayanim operations

Practical Roles Documented:

  • Checking hotels
  • Arranging local purchases
  • Supplying leads from social circles
  • Facilitating medical care
  • Providing money and logistics
  • Overt intelligence gathering
  • Rental agency owners: help Mossad rent cars without raising suspicion
  • Provide assistance of various kinds to Mossad officers operating in foreign countries

Strategic Value:

  • Low-cost deniability and reach without formal payrolls
  • Capability flagged in South Africa reporting
  • Leaked cables and investigations depict sayanim performing mundane but operationally useful tasks
  • Gives Mossad global reach without official diplomatic footprint

Controversy & Debate:

  • Ostrovsky's critics: Warning he "likely exaggerated numbers and systematization"
  • Supporters: Established reporting concedes Mossad uses diaspora contacts but disputes "more conspiratorial scale"
  • Ostrovsky's response: Claims were made public in 1990s; never legally contested

4.3 AIPAC: The Israel Lobby's Political Engine

Foundation & Purpose:

  • Founded 1954 by Isaiah L. Kenen
  • Originally "American Zionist Committee for Public Affairs" (AZCPA)
  • Renamed to American Israel Public Affairs Committee (AIPAC) in 1959
  • Pro-Israel lobbying group advocating policies to U.S. legislative and executive branches
  • Called "one of the most influential lobbying groups in U.S."

Origins: Strategic Separation:

  • Kenen was lobbyist for Israeli government (Israeli Ministry of Foreign Affairs)
  • 1953: Kenen worried he'd be investigated by State Department for not registering as "foreign agent"
  • Formed separate entity not tax-exempt that could lobby for foreign government
  • Founded partly to counter international criticism of Qibya massacre (October 1953) - Israeli troops killed at least 69 Palestinian villagers (two-thirds women/children)

Financial Scale (2024):

  • Revenue: $156,448,717 (2023–24)
  • Expenses: $100,363,669 (2023–24)
  • Endowment: $13,070,967
  • Employees: 376 (2022)
  • 2024 cycle contributions: $51,848,113
  • 2024 lobbying spending: $3,324,268
  • 2024 outside spending: $37,860,200
  • OpenSecrets: AIPAC "largest cumulative recipient" - contributions exceeded $50M (1990–2024)

Political Power Structure:

  • More than 5 million members in U.S. (as of 2025)
  • 17 regional and satellite offices
  • Headquarters on K Street, Washington, D.C.
  • Annual Policy Conference: 20,000 delegates (2016) - second only to State of Union address for Federal officials
  • Leadership Reception: hundreds of members of Congress attend

Historical Expansion:

  • 1973 (Yom Kippur War): Became "powerful organization" - budget soared from $300,000 (1973) to $7M+ (late 1980s)
  • Evolved from Zionist movement activists to prototypical Washington lobbying firm
  • Leaders recruited from legislative staff and federal bureaucracy

Campaign Funding Strategy:

  • Until 2021: Did not raise funds directly; members used unaffiliated PACs
  • Late 2021: Formed own political action committee and Super PAC (United Democracy Project)
  • "AIPAC-connected money": ~$200,000 in typical congressional campaign (four-year swing)
  • System of "key contacts": AIPAC can call "5 to 15" key contacts for every senator
  • AIPAC website and "AIPAC Insider" publication scrutinized by thousands of donors
  • Between 2000-2004: 50 AIPAC board members donated average of $72,000 each to campaigns/PACs

Electoral Targeting:

  • Paul Findley (R-Illinois): Defeated 1982 for showing enthusiasm for PLO's Arafat
  • Charles Percy (R-Illinois): Defeated 1984 (Senate Foreign Relations Committee chair) for supporting AWACs sale to Saudi Arabia
  • Jamaal Bowman & Cori Bush: Defeated 2024 after AIPAC spent $45.2M opposing them
  • Andy Levin: Ousted 2022 for supporting two-state solution, ending occupation
  • Marie Newman: Ousted 2022 for pro-Palestine positions

Legislative Influence:

  • "Anyone who is seen as hostile to Israel can be sure that AIPAC will direct campaign contributions to his or her political opponents"
  • Former Rep. Brian Baird: "There's a whole complex semantic code you learn... After a while, you find yourself saying and repeating it as if it were fact"
  • Mearsheimer & Walt: "The bottom line is that AIPAC, a de facto agent for a foreign government, has a stranglehold on Congress"
  • Bills supported by AIPAC "typically pass by unanimous votes"

Foreign Policy Impact:

  • 2002: Expressed intent to lobby Congress to authorize Iraq war use of force
  • 2003: Iraq War defended at AIPAC events
  • 2012: Called for "crippling" sanctions on Iran
  • Secures up to $3 billion/year in U.S. military aid for Israel
  • Israel is "largest cumulative recipient of U.S. foreign assistance since WWII"

Controversies:

  • 2005: Pentagon analyst Lawrence Franklin pleaded guilty to espionage - passed U.S. secrets to AIPAC policy director Steve Rosen and Iran analyst Keith Weissman
  • 2024: Ilhan Omar's "Benjamins" comment - about money/AIPAC; sparked national debate over Israel lobby influence
  • 2025: Several Democrats (Deborah Ross, Valerie Foushee, Morgan McGarvey, Seth Moulton) said they'd no longer accept AIPAC donations

Subsidiary Organizations:

  • American Israel Education Foundation (AIEF): 501(c)(3) charitable arm
  • Conducts all-expenses-paid trips to Israel for Congress
  • 2005 alone: more than 100 members of Congress visited Israel, some multiple times
  • "Top spender on congressional travel" in years when trips occur (every two years)
