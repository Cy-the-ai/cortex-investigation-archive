BANKING & FEDERAL RESERVE

Overview

This priority investigates the creation of the U.S. Federal Reserve System, the international banking families involved in its establishment, and the broader network of central banking influence that shaped 20th and 21st century monetary policy. Research reveals coordinated efforts by banking dynasties to establish centralized control over national money and credit, culminating in the secret Jekyll Island meeting of 1910 and subsequent Federal Reserve Act of 1913.

Key Finding: The Federal Reserve System emerged from a highly secretive process dominated by a small group of interconnected banking elites. Despite claims of "decentralization," the system centralized monetary control in New York financial interests while creating an illusion of regional distribution. Banking families across Europe and America maintained close ties through marriage alliances, business partnerships, and coordinated policy advocacy.

1. Jekyll Island Conference (1910)

The Secret Meeting

Date: November 1910
Location: Jekyll Island Club, Georgia
Duration: 9-10 days
Attendees: Six men, later called the "First Name Club"

Participants (first names only for secrecy):

  • Nelson Aldrich — Senator (R-Rhode Island), Chair of National Monetary Commission, Rockefeller in-law
  • Frank Vanderlip — President, National City Bank (Rockefeller-controlled)
  • Paul Warburg — Partner, Kuhn Loeb & Co., German-born banking expert
  • Henry Davison — Senior Partner, J.P. Morgan Company
  • Charles Norton — President, First National Bank (J.P. Morgan-controlled)
  • A. Piatt Andrew — Assistant Secretary of Treasury, Harvard economist
  • Arthur Shelton — Aldrich's private secretary

Benjamin Strong — Vice President, Bankers Trust (later first NY Fed Governor) — Frequently consulted but attendance disputed

Secrecy Measures

Transport: Private railroad car from Hoboken, New Jersey with blinds drawn
Cover story: Duck hunting expedition
Communication: First names only ("Nelson, Harry, Frank, Paul, Piatt, Arthur")
Staff: All club servants given two-week vacation; new staff brought from mainland who did not know identities
Training: Staff instructed not to identify anyone by last name, ever

From Frank Vanderlip's autobiography: "Despite my views about the value to society of greater publicity for the affairs of corporations, there was an occasion near the close of 1910, when I was as secretive, indeed, as furtive, as any conspirator."

The Plan

Key Features of Jekyll Island Draft:

  • Central bank with 15 branches (later 12 regional banks)
  • Each branch governed by boards elected by member banks
  • Larger banks get more votes (wealth-weighted governance)
  • Branches hold reserves, issue currency, discount commercial paper, clear checks
  • National body sets discount rates and buys/sells securities

Disagreement at Jekyll Island:

  • Aldrich: Wanted straightforward central bank on European model
  • Warburg: Insisted "decentralized" structure needed for political acceptance (duplicitous camouflage)
  • Result: Warburg's "decentralization" deception won the day

3. Federal Reserve Act of 1913

Legislative Process

From Jekyll Island to Federal Reserve Act:

  • 1910: Jekyll Island meeting (Aldrich Plan drafted)
  • 1911: Aldrich presented to National Monetary Commission; rejected by Democrats
  • 1912: Republicans lost Congress/White House (Wilson elected); Aldrich Plan shelved
  • 1913: Carter Glass & Robert Owen introduce Democratic bill with same technical structure

Paul Warburg's Role:

  • Consulted directly by Glass, Owen, and Wilson's advisers (House, Morgenthau, McAdoo)
  • "Technical expertise was respected by Democratic and Republican politicians alike"
  • Final Federal Reserve Act "closely resembled" Aldrich Plan (Warburg's line-by-line comparison in his 1750-page book)

Major Changes from Aldrich Plan:

  • Political structure: Board of Governors appointed by President (not elected by banks)
  • Decision-making: More "democratic" in appearance
  • Technical structure: Virtually identical to Jekyll Island draft

Structure of Federal Reserve System

Board of Governors (Washington D.C.):

  • 7 members appointed by President, confirmed by Senate (14-year terms)
  • Federal Advisory Council: 12 bankers chosen by regional Reserve Banks
  • Key Issue: Advisory Council wields influence over monetary policy (Warburg's design)

Regional Reserve Banks (12 districts):

  • New York (largest), Boston, Philadelphia, Cleveland, Richmond, Atlanta, Chicago, St. Louis, Minneapolis, Kansas City, Dallas, San Francisco
  • Each governed by board of directors (9 members: 6 from member banks, 3 public interest)
  • Wealth-weighted voting: Larger banks have more control

Key Power Concentration:

  • Federal Reserve Bank of New York — De facto most powerful (largest reserves, proximity to Wall Street)
  • First NY Fed Governor: Benjamin Strong (Morgan lieutenant)
  • Mullins' Charge: "Warburg's plan was a central bank plan, fulfilling main functions of a central bank; it would be owned by private individuals who would profit from ownership of shares"

Causes:

  • Gold movements by international bankers in London
  • Contraction of money supply

Causes:

  • Public outcry for monetary system stabilization

6. Monetary Policy Influence

Interest Rate Manipulation

Federal Funds Rate:

  • Primary tool for economic influence
  • Controlled by Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC)
  • Criticism: Favors financial sector over real economy

**Quantitative Easing (QE) — 2008-2014

QE1 (2008-2010):

  • Fed balance sheet: $800B → $2.3 trillion
  • Assets purchased: Treasury bonds, mortgage-backed securities (MBS)
  • Purpose: Lower long-term interest rates, stimulate lending

QE2 (2010-2011):

  • Additional $600B in asset purchases
  • Focus: Treasury securities

QE3 (2012-2014):

  • $1.6 trillion in additional purchases
  • Fed balance sheet peaked at ~$4.5 trillion

Criticism of QE:

  • Asset Inflation: Stock market, housing prices rose while wages stagnated
  • Wall Street Beneficiaries: Banks received liquidity without increased lending to Main Street
  • Wealth Transfer: Asset holders (wealthy) benefited; savers (poor) hurt through inflation
  • Unintended Consequences: Increased wealth inequality

2008 Financial Crisis Response:

  • Fed cut federal funds rate from 5.25% (Sept 2007) to near zero (Dec 2008)
  • Emergency lending: Discount window, TARP coordination, commercial paper funding facility
  • Heritage Foundation: "Fed facilitated bailouts to financially troubled institutions by invoking its emergency lending authority"

9. Public Debt & Money Creation

Federal Reserve Notes vs. U.S. Notes

Before 1971: U.S. Treasury issued "United States Notes" (direct government debt)
After 1971: Federal Reserve issues "Federal Reserve Notes" (debt owed to Federal Reserve System)

Money Creation Process:

  1. Treasury borrows from Federal Reserve
  2. Federal Reserve creates Federal Reserve Notes
  3. Treasury pays interest on borrowed money
  4. Interest paid to Federal Reserve member banks (dividends)
