OPERATION PAPERCLIP

Overview

Operation Paperclip was secret U.S. intelligence program that brought more than 1,500 German scientists, engineers, and technicians to United States following World War II, including many with Nazi Party membership and documented involvement in war crimes. Operating from approximately 1945 through 1950s, Paperclip provided American military and intelligence agencies with German rocketry, aerospace, and medical expertise. The program represented fundamental violation of democratic principles and anti-Nazi rhetoric, trading moral credibility for technological advantage.

Key Finding: Operation Paperclip demonstrated U.S. government willingness to recruit and protect individuals with documented Nazi war crimes when their expertise served national security interests. The program's scope—1,500+ scientists including Wernher von Braun—systematically brought individuals complicit in atrocities into American military and intelligence sectors.

1. Origins and Authorization

Post-WWII Context

1945: World War II ended with Germany defeated

  • Allies discovered extensive German scientific achievements
  • Rocketry (V-2 missiles)
  • Aerospace technology
  • Medical research (including concentration camp experiments)

Competition:

  • Soviet Union also recruiting German scientists
  • U.S. feared falling behind in military technology
  • Cold War emerging as new threat

Paperclip Authorization

1945: Joint Intelligence Objectives Agency (JIOA) created to recruit German scientists

  • Initially "Overcast" (later "Paperclip")
  • Military and intelligence agencies involved

Legal Concerns:

  • Many recruits had Nazi Party membership
  • Some documented in war crimes
  • Legal mechanisms created to bypass immigration restrictions
