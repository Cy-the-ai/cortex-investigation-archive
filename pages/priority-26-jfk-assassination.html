JFK ASSASSINATION

Overview

The assassination of President John F. Kennedy on November 22, 1963, in Dallas, Texas, represents one of the most consequential and controversial events in American history. While the Warren Commission concluded that Lee Harvey Oswald acted alone, the House Select Committee on Assassinations (HSCA) in 1979 found "high probability" that two gunmen fired and that Kennedy was "probably assassinated as a result of a conspiracy." The assassination occurred amidst intense conflict between Kennedy and the CIA over covert operations, particularly Operation Mongoose targeting Cuba, raising questions about agency involvement. Decades of document releases, investigations, and research have failed to resolve fundamental questions about the events in Dealey Plaza.

Key Finding: The JFK assassination cannot be separated from the broader context of CIA-Kennedy conflict over covert operations, the agency's record of political assassinations abroad, and the pattern of intelligence agencies operating beyond legal constraints. The Warren Commission's rushed investigation, the HSCA's contradictory findings, and ongoing questions about Oswald's activities and connections create legitimate suspicion of a broader conspiracy involving intelligence agency elements.

2. The Warren Commission

Commission Process

Duration: Approximately 10 months (November 1963 to September 1964)

Key Finding: Lee Harvey Oswald acted alone in assassinating President Kennedy

Evidence Supporting Lone Gunman Conclusion:

  • Oswald's rifle found in Book Depository
  • Oswald's palm print on rifle
  • Oswald's purchase of rifle
  • Bullet fragments matched Oswald's rifle
  • No evidence of additional shooters

The Magic Bullet Theory

Official Account:

  • One bullet (Commission Exhibit 399) struck Kennedy in back, exited through throat
  • Same bullet then struck Connally, entering back, exiting chest, entering wrist, lodging in thigh
  • Bullet found on Connally's stretcher in nearly pristine condition

Criticism:

  • Pristine condition after passing through two bodies and breaking bone
  • Trajectory challenges
  • Bullet's behavior inconsistent with physics of shooting

Warren Commission Response:

  • Bullet tumbling and deformation less than expected
  • Tissue cushioning effects

Oswald's Background

Biographical Details:

  • Born October 18, 1939, in New Orleans
  • U.S. Marine Corps veteran (1956-1959)
  • Defected to Soviet Union (1959-1962)
  • Returned to U.S. with Russian wife Marina
  • Attempted defection to Cuba (rejected by Cuban embassy in Mexico City, 1963)

CIA Monitoring:

  • CIA maintained files on Oswald after defection to Soviet Union
  • CIA had recorded two tapped telephone calls to Soviet embassy by a man identified as Oswald who spoke in "broken Russian" (though real Oswald was fluent)
  • Files indicate Oswald was subject of monitoring

Mexico City Visit:

  • Oswald visited Cuban and Soviet embassies in Mexico City (September-October 1963)
  • Photographed at Soviet embassy
  • CIA surveillance during visit

Chronology Analysis: Oswald's contacts with Soviet and Cuban embassies, defection to Soviet Union, and potential connections to intelligence communities (through these contacts) create ambiguity about whether he was lone gunman or potentially recruited or used as asset.

Warren Commission Criticisms

Limited Scope:

  • Did not fully investigate Oswald's intelligence contacts
  • Did not adequately explore potential connections to organized crime
  • Did not investigate Jack Ruby's background and connections

4. House Select Committee on Assassinations (1976-1979)

Committee Formation

Established: 1976
Mandate: Investigate assassinations of Kennedy, Martin Luther King Jr., and Robert F. Kennedy

Scope:

  • Broader than Warren Commission
  • Access to evidence not available to Warren Commission
  • Scientific analysis of evidence

Acoustic Evidence

Dictabelt Recording:

  • Dallas police officer H.B. McLain's motorcycle microphone channel recorded during shooting
  • Scientific analysis revealed acoustic signatures suggesting additional gunshots

HSCA Findings on Acoustics:

  • "Scientific acoustical evidence establishes a high probability that two gunmen fired at President John F. Kennedy."
  • Four acoustic impulses identified as probable gunfire
  • Pattern consistent with shots from grassy knoll area

Reliability Debate:

  • Acoustic evidence criticized for reliability
  • National Academy of Sciences challenged methodology
  • However, HSCA found sufficient evidence to conclude probable conspiracy

HSCA Final Conclusions

1979 Report:

  • "President Kennedy was probably assassinated as a result of a conspiracy by unidentified individuals."
  • "Scientific acoustic evidence establishes a high probability that two gunmen fired at President John F. Kennedy."
  • Did not identify specific conspirators
  • Did not rule out involvement of organized crime or anti-Castro groups

Contradiction with Warren Commission:

  • Warren: Oswald acted alone
  • HSCA: Probable conspiracy by unidentified individuals
  • Official government conclusions conflicted

Chronology of Investigations

1964: Warren Commission concludes lone gunman
1979: HSCA concludes probable conspiracy (15 years later)
1979: Reliability of acoustic evidence came under broad criticism

Chronology Analysis: The 15-year gap between investigations, new evidence becoming available, and contradictory conclusions illustrate how official narratives can shift over time while maintaining denial of specific conspiracy involvement.
